# Datum as a service

## What it is sold as

Not a model that tells a firm its AI savings. That framing leans on the weakest
part, the projected saving, and a finance buyer will pull that thread first.

It is sold as a structured AI-readiness diagnostic, with Datum as the instrument.
The firm is buying a defensible map of where AI helps in their practice, what it
is worth, and in what order to act, produced from their own data through a method
they can see and challenge.

## Why the elicitation is the value, not a weakness

Parts of the model are measured and parts are elicited. The cost base is measured
from the firm's timesheets. The classification is read against a published
framework. The saving rates, the tool autonomy and the adoption assumptions are
elicited in a workshop.

The elicitation is the billable work, not something to hide. The firm is paying
for the structured conversation that turns scattered intuition into a single
defensible map, and for the judgement that frames their work against the
framework and the 2026 trajectory. Vester-style methods are built on elicited
estimates handled exactly this way, so eliciting and labelling the soft inputs is
the method working as intended, not a gap in it.

## Inputs the firm provides

- Timesheet export: hours by band by stage. This makes the cost base real.
- Resourcing and band cost rates, salary based. This costs the hours.
- A half-day workshop with people who know how the work actually flows, to elicit
  the tool autonomy, the adoption assumptions and the work-flow and influence
  between stages.

## Method

1. Classify the firm's work against the framework (the task by stage grid).
2. Derive the groupings from their data rather than imposing them (RNM blocks).
3. Cost each stage from the timesheets and band rates.
4. Map their work onto the 2026 trajectory, where the tooling has moved and what
   kind of computation and autonomy it now reaches.
5. Run the workshop to elicit autonomy, adoption and the work-flow matrices.
6. Produce the deliverables and the conversion decision.

## Deliverables

- The opportunity and readiness maps: where AI helps, and whether the tooling is
  there yet.
- A banded and explicitly conditional business case: cost per stage measured, the
  capacity released as a low, expected and high projection, never a single number.
- The conversion decision: released capacity is hours, not money, and books value
  only through the margin route (run the work with fewer hours) or the growth
  route (re-sell freed time as fee). The output is a decision, not a promise.
- A sequenced roadmap: act now where capable tooling runs on project data, watch
  where the value or the tooling is not there yet, and needs foundations where the
  AI needs the firm's own data in order first.
- The data-readiness recommendation.

## The land and the expand

The diagnostic is the wedge. Its honest conclusion, that the firm cannot reach the
cognitive and agentic tooling without its own data in a usable state, sells the
larger and stickier engagement, which is the data infrastructure itself. The
needs-foundations lane on the roadmap is where that conversation starts.

So the commercial shape is a fixed-scope diagnostic of a few weeks as the land,
and the data platform plus implementation support as the expand. Price the
diagnostic low enough that a managing partner can approve it without a committee,
and let it qualify whether the firm is serious before committing to the build.

## Risks to manage

- Timesheet data in these firms is messy and politically guarded. Budget time to
  clean it and agree the band mapping up front.
- The false-precision risk sinks trust on first contact if you lead with the
  pound figure. Lead with the measured cost base and the structure, and show the
  single saving number last and most heavily caveated.
- The named tools date quickly. They are perishable evidence under the durable
  computation-type and autonomy structure, and need a refresh cadence.
